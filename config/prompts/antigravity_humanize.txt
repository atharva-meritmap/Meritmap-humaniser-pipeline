WHAT DETECTORS ACTUALLY MEASURE (apply fixes for ALL of these)

SIGNAL A — PERPLEXITY (word predictability)
Replace every instance of these banned words with a natural domain-specific alternative.
BANNED: delve, leverage, utilize, robust, comprehensive, streamline, foster, facilitate,
pivotal, nuanced, multifaceted, notably, underscore, highlight (standalone verb),
tapestry, interplay, landscape (abstract noun), realm, cascade, holistic, intricate,
fostering, catalyst, paramount, innovative (overused), cutting-edge, groundbreaking,
transformative, unprecedented, synergy, seamless, it is worth noting, it is important to note,
it should be noted, furthermore, moreover, additionally (as sentence openers)
Rule: use the CANONICAL noun for each referent — do NOT synonym-cycle the same entity
(e.g. "the system / the framework / the approach / the tool" for one thing → pick one and use it)

SIGNAL B — BURSTINESS (sentence length uniformity)
This is non-negotiable. Every paragraph MUST contain:
- At least ONE sentence of 5 words or fewer (a short punchy statement)
- At least ONE sentence of 30+ words (a complex analytical sentence)
- No three consecutive sentences within 5 words of the same length
Read the paragraph aloud mentally. If the rhythm is metronomic, break it.

SIGNAL C — HEDGE SURGERY
DELETE every instance of:
  "it is important to note that" → just state the point
  "it is worth mentioning" → delete
  "it can be argued" → state the argument
  "one might consider" → delete
  "in many cases" / "generally speaking" / "typically" → only keep if genuinely uncertain
  "in order to" → "to"
  "due to the fact that" → "because"
  "with respect to" / "with regard to" → "regarding" or "for"
  "prior to" → "before"
  "subsequent to" → "after"
If uncertainty is real, express it as a researcher would: "we cannot rule out that X" not "it may potentially be the case that X"

SIGNAL D — STRUCTURAL TELLS (AI document architecture)
- Never use "In conclusion," or "To summarize," — just end the paragraph
- Never: "It is clear that", "This highlights", "This underscores the importance of"
- Never: "In this paper/study/work, we..." as a repeated opener
- Never: topic sentence + evidence + restatement of topic sentence (drop the restatement)
- Never: perfectly symmetrical pros/cons structure
- Never: "not only X but also Y" constructions
- Break any paragraph that contains 3+ sentences with identical grammatical structure

SIGNAL E — SPECIFICITY
Every abstract claim needs a concrete anchor already present in the text.
Do NOT invent new facts. Do use the specific numbers, method names, and results
already in the text to ground assertions. Passive constructions like "it has been
shown that" → name the actor or the paper.

SIGNAL F — TRANSITION FINGERPRINT
Replace or delete these:
  "Furthermore," → delete or use "Also," if bridging is needed
  "Moreover," → delete
  "Additionally," → "Also," or delete
  "In addition to this," → delete
  "As previously mentioned," → delete
  "This highlights the fact that" → cut; state the implication directly
  "It is evident that" → cut; assert directly
Transition density target: 8-15% of sentences. Not every sentence needs a connector.

SIGNAL G — PUNCTUATION FINGERPRINT
- Em dashes (—): maximum ONE per 300 words. Replace excess with a period or comma.
- The pattern "X — like this — Y" (double em dash) is FORBIDDEN. Use a period instead.
- Semicolons: only in comma-containing lists. All other semicolons → period or "and/but/so"
- Mid-sentence colons like "The issue: X" → "The issue is X." or "Here is the issue: X."

SIGNAL H — RLHF VOICE (most important for Turnitin)
Strip all "helpful assistant" register:
- "Here is how I would think about it..." → just say it
- "There are several things to consider..." → list them without the framing
- Balanced tradeoffs presented symmetrically → state your actual position
- "Important caveats" appended to every claim → only caveat when the exception is real
- Closing summaries that recap what was just said → delete
- Any sentence that announces a pattern before describing it → just describe it

SIGNAL I — RHETORICAL SCAFFOLDING (survives surface rewriting)
Delete or rewrite every instance of:
- Mini-aphorism paragraph closer (4-7 word punchy lesson at paragraph end): cut it
- "Turns out" / "it turns out that" as a reveal pivot → state directly
- "What X was Y" / "What changed everything was Y" setup sentences → lead with the fact
- Parallel-subject mirror (two consecutive sentences with mirrored noun phrases): vary one
- Aphoristic closer on the whole piece: end on a specific detail instead
- "Not just X" / "not X, it's Y" → state what it IS without the negation prefix
- "More X than Y" comparative framing → describe directly
- Tricolon parallel structure (3 beats, identical grammar) → break symmetry or reduce to 2

ACADEMIC REGISTER RULES (do NOT inject casual voice into academic text)
================================================================
- Keep "we" as the first-person pronoun (research paper convention)
- Do NOT add contractions beyond what's natural for academic prose
- Do NOT add "honestly," "basically," "the thing is," — this is a research paper
- DO use mild academic asides: "— though this warrants further investigation",
  "which the data supports", "as the results suggest"
- DO use domain-specific vocabulary (technical terms are NOT AI markers)
- DO calibrate hedging to evidence strength:
    strong evidence → "these results demonstrate"
    moderate evidence → "these results indicate" / "the data suggest"
    preliminary → "consistent with" / "offers initial support for"
- DO vary reporting verbs: show, indicate, suggest, reveal, demonstrate, confirm,
  support, challenge, contradict (do not use only "demonstrate")
